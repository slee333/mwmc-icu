1. Abstract
   Background: Acute Hypoxic Respiratory Failure (AHRF) remains a leading cause of morbidity and mortality among critically ill patients, with clinical decision-making often constrained by time pressure, information overload, and cognitive burden. Recent advances in large language models (LLMs) have demonstrated promising capabilities in clinical reasoning and medical knowledge synthesis. However, no prospective randomized trial has evaluated the impact of integrating LLM-generated clinical decision support into real-time intensive care unit (ICU) workflows.
   Methods: We propose a prospective, block-randomized controlled trial conducted across the Medical Intensive Care Unit (MICU) and Coronary Care Unit (CCU) at MetroWest Medical Center and St. Vincent Hospital. Adult patients (age ≥18 years) admitted from the Emergency Department with a diagnosis of AHRF (PaO2/FiO2 ratio ≤300 mmHg) requiring supplemental oxygen or ventilatory support will be eligible for enrollment. Subjects will be randomized to either standard clinical care (Control Arm) or standard care augmented by LLM-generated diagnostic and therapeutic recommendations (Intervention Arm). In the Intervention Arm, de-identified clinical data will be submitted to a validated LLM via a secure, HIPAA-compliant research platform, and the resulting outputs—including differential diagnoses, treatment plans, ventilator management guidance, and clinical deterioration risk assessments—will be presented to the treating clinical team for integration into their assessment and plan.
   Outcomes: The primary endpoint is in-hospital all-cause mortality. Secondary endpoints include ICU and total hospital length of stay, ventilator-free days at day 28, time to care escalation, incidence of ventilator-associated pneumonia (VAP) and acute respiratory distress syndrome (ARDS), and total duration of antibiotic and corticosteroid therapies.
   Significance: This study will provide the first prospective, randomized evidence on the clinical utility of LLM-augmented decision support in the ICU setting, with implications for the broader integration of artificial intelligence into critical care medicine.
2. Specific Aims
   The overarching goal of this research is to determine whether real-time integration of a large language model into the ICU clinical workflow improves patient outcomes in acute hypoxic respiratory failure. We propose the following specific aims:

Specific Aim 1: To evaluate the effect of LLM-augmented clinical decision support on in-hospital all-cause mortality among ICU patients presenting with AHRF, compared with standard care alone.
Specific Aim 2: To assess the impact of LLM integration on secondary clinical outcomes including ICU and hospital length of stay, ventilator-free days, time to care escalation, and rates of clinical complications (VAP, ARDS).
Specific Aim 3: To characterize the nature and frequency of LLM-induced changes in clinical decision-making through structured provider surveys, thereby elucidating the mechanisms by which AI-generated recommendations influence diagnostic and therapeutic reasoning at the point of care. 3. Background and Significance
3.1. Clinical Burden of Acute Hypoxic Respiratory Failure
Acute hypoxic respiratory failure is among the most common reasons for admission to the intensive care unit and is associated with substantial morbidity, prolonged hospitalization, and high mortality rates. The heterogeneity of underlying etiologies—ranging from pneumonia and cardiogenic pulmonary edema to acute respiratory distress syndrome—demands rapid diagnostic precision and dynamic therapeutic adjustment. Delayed or inaccurate clinical decisions in this patient population can lead to preventable deterioration, unnecessary intubation, and increased healthcare utilization.
3.2. Cognitive Burden and Diagnostic Challenges in the ICU
Intensivists and ICU clinicians operate under conditions of extreme cognitive load, managing complex, multi-system disease processes with incomplete and rapidly evolving clinical data. Diagnostic error in the ICU has been reported at rates ranging from 5% to 28%, with contributing factors including information overload, fatigue, anchoring bias, and premature closure. These cognitive vulnerabilities represent a meaningful target for augmentation through artificial intelligence systems capable of synthesizing large volumes of clinical information and generating structured, evidence-based recommendations.
3.3. Large Language Models in Clinical Medicine
Large language models represent a class of generative artificial intelligence systems trained on extensive corpora of text data, including biomedical literature, clinical guidelines, and electronic health record documentation. Recent evaluations have demonstrated that state-of-the-art LLMs achieve performance comparable to or exceeding that of medical professionals on standardized medical examinations and clinical reasoning benchmarks. However, the translation of these capabilities from controlled benchmark evaluations to prospective clinical application remains largely unexplored.
To date, the majority of studies evaluating LLMs in healthcare have been retrospective in design, relying on curated clinical vignettes rather than real-time patient data. The proposed study addresses this critical gap by prospectively evaluating LLM-generated clinical decision support within the actual ICU workflow, using de-identified patient data transmitted through a secure, purpose-built research platform.
3.4. Innovation and Significance
This trial is, to our knowledge, the first prospective randomized controlled study to evaluate the clinical impact of LLM integration at the point of care in an ICU setting. By embedding AI-generated recommendations directly into the clinical decision-making process—while preserving physician autonomy through a human-in-the-loop design—this study will generate actionable evidence regarding the safety, efficacy, and practical feasibility of AI-augmented critical care. The findings will inform institutional policies, regulatory frameworks, and future multi-center trials in this rapidly evolving domain. 4. Research Design and Methods
4.1. Study Design
This is a prospective, block-randomized, controlled, single-center (two-campus) clinical trial. Eligible subjects will be allocated to one of two parallel arms: a Control Arm receiving standard clinical care, and an Intervention Arm receiving standard care augmented by LLM-generated clinical decision support. The study will employ a pragmatic design intended to evaluate the real-world effectiveness of LLM integration within the existing ICU workflow infrastructure.
4.2. Study Setting
The trial will be conducted in the Medical Intensive Care Units (MICU) and Coronary Care Units (CCU) at MetroWest Medical Center and St. Vincent Hospital, both affiliated with Tenet Healthcare Corporation. These units serve a diverse patient population and manage a broad spectrum of critical illness etiologies, providing a generalizable clinical environment for the proposed evaluation.
4.3. Study Population and Eligibility Criteria
4.3.1. Inclusion Criteria
Patients must satisfy all of the following criteria to be eligible for enrollment:
Age ≥18 years at the time of ICU admission.
Admission to the MICU or CCU directly from the Emergency Department with a primary or contributing diagnosis of acute hypoxic respiratory failure.
Documented PaO2/FiO2 ratio of ≤300 mmHg on admission arterial blood gas or within 6 hours of ICU arrival.
Requirement for supplemental oxygen therapy (nasal cannula, high-flow nasal cannula, non-invasive positive pressure ventilation) or invasive mechanical ventilation at the time of enrollment.
4.3.2. Exclusion Criteria
Patients meeting any of the following criteria will be excluded:
Presence of a Do Not Intubate (DNI) or Do Not Resuscitate (DNR) order documented at the time of ICU admission.
Anticipated mortality within 48 hours of admission, as determined by the attending intensivist.
Pregnancy (confirmed or suspected).
Transfer from an outside hospital with >24 hours of prior ICU-level treatment.
Known prisoner or ward of the state status.
Prior enrollment in this study during a previous ICU admission.
4.4. Randomization and Allocation Concealment
Eligible subjects will be randomized using a computer-generated block randomization scheme with variable block sizes (4, 6, and 8), stratified by study site. The randomization sequence will be generated prior to study initiation and maintained within a secure, web-based research management platform accessible only to designated research team members. Allocation concealment will be ensured through the use of sequentially numbered, opaque assignments generated by the platform at the time of enrollment. Given the nature of the intervention, blinding of the clinical team to group allocation following randomization is not feasible; however, outcome assessors will be blinded to group assignment.
4.5. Study Procedures and Intervention Protocol
4.5.1. Enrollment and Initial Assessment
Upon identification of an eligible patient, the on-call research team member will be notified by the ICU clinical team. The research team member will verify eligibility using a standardized electronic screening questionnaire within the study management platform. Following confirmation of eligibility and randomization, the clinical team (house officer and attending physician) will complete a standard Physician History and Physical Examination (H&P) and formulate a preliminary Assessment and Plan, as per routine clinical practice.
4.5.2. Data Extraction and De-identification (Intervention Arm Only)
For subjects randomized to the Intervention Arm, the following standardized data extraction protocol will be executed:
The preliminary Assessment and Plan will be temporarily withheld from the documentation workflow.
A designated research team member will extract the relevant clinical information from the H&P and associated diagnostic data (laboratory values, imaging interpretations, vital sign trends).
All extracted data will undergo a double-screening de-identification process to remove all 18 HIPAA-defined protected health information (PHI) identifiers prior to export from the hospital’s secure electronic health record (EHR) system.
The de-identified clinical summary will be entered into the study’s secure web-based platform, which serves as the interface for LLM query submission.
4.5.3. LLM Output Generation
The de-identified clinical data will be submitted to a validated large language model via a secure application programming interface (API) connection. The LLM will be prompted using a standardized, IRB-approved clinical reasoning template designed to elicit:
The five most probable differential diagnoses, rank-ordered by estimated likelihood, with supporting clinical rationale for each.
A five-point evidence-based therapeutic plan addressing the primary diagnosis and immediate management priorities.
Recommendations regarding airway management, including indications for intubation and suggested initial ventilator parameters.
Risk stratification for clinical deterioration within 24–48 hours, with identification of specific clinical parameters warranting escalation of care.

The specific LLM utilized may include, but is not limited to, models such as GPT-4, GPT-4o, Claude, or other validated clinical-grade language models available at the time of study execution. Model selection and version will be documented for each enrolled subject. The standardized prompt template will be maintained consistently across all subjects regardless of the underlying model.
4.5.4. Clinical Integration and Decision Feedback
The LLM-generated output will be presented to the treating clinical team (resident physician and attending intensivist) through the study platform interface. The clinical team will then:
Review the AI-generated differential diagnoses and therapeutic recommendations alongside their independently formulated preliminary Assessment and Plan.
Formulate a final, integrated Assessment and Plan, incorporating or rejecting LLM recommendations at their clinical discretion.
Complete a structured decision-impact questionnaire documenting whether and how the LLM output influenced their clinical reasoning, diagnostic considerations, or therapeutic decisions.

All final clinical decisions will remain under the sole authority of the treating physician team. The LLM output serves exclusively as a decision-support adjunct and does not constitute a clinical order or replace physician judgment. 5. Outcome Measures

6.1. Analysis Populations
All outcome analyses will be conducted using both intention-to-treat (ITT) and per-protocol populations. The ITT population will include all randomized subjects regardless of protocol adherence. The per-protocol population will include subjects who completed the study procedures without major protocol deviations.
6.2. Primary Outcome Analysis
In-hospital mortality will be compared between study arms using the chi-square test (or Fisher’s exact test if expected cell counts are <5). Multivariable logistic regression will be employed to estimate the adjusted odds ratio, controlling for pre-specified covariates including age, sex, baseline PaO₂/FiO₂ ratio, APACHE II score, and presence of major comorbidities.
6.3. Secondary Outcome Analyses
Continuous outcome variables (length of stay, ventilator-free days, antibiotic and corticosteroid duration) will be compared using independent two-sample t-tests or Mann-Whitney U tests as appropriate based on distributional assessment via Shapiro-Wilk testing. Time-to-event outcomes (time to care escalation, time to intubation) will be analyzed using Kaplan-Meier survival estimation and Cox proportional hazards regression models, with treatment arm as the primary predictor and adjustment for baseline covariates.
6.4. Subgroup Analyses
Pre-specified subgroup analyses will evaluate treatment effect heterogeneity across the following strata: underlying etiology of respiratory failure (infectious vs. non-infectious), patient age (≤65 vs. >65 years), body mass index (<30 vs. ≥30 kg/m²), smoking history, baseline hypoxia severity (mild vs. moderate vs. severe per Berlin criteria), and presence of specific comorbidities including coronary artery disease, heart failure, diabetes mellitus, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease, interstitial lung disease, and chronic kidney disease. Interaction terms will be included in regression models to formally test for effect modification.
6.5. Sample Size Considerations
Formal sample size estimation will be based on available preliminary data regarding ICU mortality rates in the study population. Assuming a baseline in-hospital mortality rate of 25–30% among AHRF patients and a clinically meaningful absolute risk reduction of 10–15%, a two-sided alpha of 0.05, and 80% power, we estimate that approximately 150–200 subjects per arm (300–400 total) will be required. An interim analysis will be planned at 50% enrollment to reassess assumptions and permit early stopping for futility or overwhelming efficacy. 7. Data Management and Security
7.1. Data Collection Platform
All study data will be collected, managed, and stored using a purpose-built, web-based research management platform developed specifically for this trial. The platform will serve as the central hub for eligibility screening, randomization, de-identified data entry, LLM query submission and output retrieval, and provider questionnaire administration. Data will be exported to a structured, access-controlled repository (e.g., Google Workspace with institutional security configurations) to facilitate collaborative data review among research team members.
7.2. Data De-identification and Privacy Protections
All clinical data transmitted to the LLM will undergo rigorous double-screening de-identification to remove all 18 categories of protected health information as defined by the HIPAA Privacy Rule Safe Harbor method (§164.514(b)(2)). No patient identifiers, including name, medical record number, date of birth, or any other directly or indirectly identifying information, will be included in data transmitted outside the hospital’s secure network. A crosswalk file linking study identification numbers to patient medical record numbers will be maintained in an encrypted, access-restricted database accessible only to the Principal Investigator and designated research personnel.
7.3. HIPAA Compliance
This study will comply with all applicable provisions of the Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act (HIPAA) of 1996 and the HITECH Act. LLM API connections will be established through HIPAA-compliant infrastructure with data encryption in transit (TLS 1.2+) and at rest (AES-256). No PHI will be stored on the LLM provider’s servers. API configurations will be set to disable data retention and model training on submitted inputs where such options are available. 8. Ethical Considerations and Human Subjects Protections
8.1. Institutional Review Board Approval
This study will be submitted for review and approval by the Institutional Review Board (IRB) at [Institution Name] prior to enrollment of any subjects. All study procedures will be conducted in accordance with the ethical principles of the Declaration of Helsinki (2013 revision) and applicable federal regulations (45 CFR 46, 21 CFR 50/56).
8.2. Informed Consent
Given the minimal additional risk posed by the intervention—which provides supplementary clinical decision support without altering the ultimate authority of the treating physician—a waiver of informed consent will be requested under 45 CFR 46.116(f). Justification for the waiver will include: (a) the research involves no more than minimal risk to subjects; (b) the waiver will not adversely affect the rights and welfare of subjects; (c) the research could not practicably be carried out without the waiver due to the time-critical nature of ICU admission decisions; and (d) subjects or their legally authorized representatives will be provided with pertinent information after participation through a post-enrollment notification process.
8.3. Risk-Benefit Assessment
The primary risk associated with this study is the theoretical possibility that an LLM-generated recommendation, if followed uncritically, could lead to a suboptimal clinical decision. This risk is mitigated by the human-in-the-loop study design, in which all LLM outputs are reviewed by the clinical team and no AI-generated recommendation is implemented without explicit physician approval. The anticipated benefits include improved diagnostic accuracy, enhanced therapeutic planning, and reduction in cognitive burden on the clinical team, potentially translating to improved patient outcomes. 9. Research Management Platform
A dedicated web-based research management platform will be developed and deployed to support all operational aspects of this trial. The platform will integrate the following functions into a unified, user-friendly interface:
Eligibility Screening Module: A structured electronic questionnaire implementing all inclusion and exclusion criteria, with automated logic to determine enrollment eligibility in real time.
Randomization Engine: Computer-generated block randomization with allocation concealment, executed at the time of enrollment and recorded with audit trail logging.
Subject Identification: Automated assignment of unique study identification numbers with sequential tracking across both study sites.
LLM Interface: A secure data entry module for de-identified H&P submission, with integrated API connectivity to the designated LLM. Outputs will be displayed within the platform for immediate clinical team review.
Provider Survey Module: Integrated structured questionnaires for capturing decision-impact data and clinician feedback, with automated follow-up email distribution at 24 hours post-enrollment.
Data Export: Automated export of all study data to a shared, access-controlled repository for collaborative analysis. 10. Projected Timeline
